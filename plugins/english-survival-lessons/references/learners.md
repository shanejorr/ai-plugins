# Learners

Two newly adopted Colombian teens taught at home by Shane (advanced Spanish, native
English) for ~20 school days before they start US public school in Cobb County, GA.
The school will provide intensive ESOL once they enroll; the home course aims at
**functional survival English** for daily US life and a school day, not fluency.

Both children get the **same lesson and the same media each day**. Differentiation
appears only as teacher notes inside the lesson plan, never as separate materials.

## Luciana — age 16, going into US high school

- Low-beginner English. First language Spanish (Colombian).
- Academically strong, responsible, organized; usually a top-5 student in Colombia.
- Comfortable with authority; adapts well; empathetic.
- Has a diagnosed childhood emotional disorder; can get irritable when she feels
  falsely accused or is dealing with the disorder, but calms quickly.
- Tends to "mother" / correct her younger brother — discourage this in lessons.
- Interests: volleyball, music (reggaeton), fashion, time with friends.

## Camilo — age 13, going into US middle school

- Low-beginner English. First language Spanish (Colombian).
- Average IQ. School underperformance driven by behavior / carelessness, not ability.
- History of ADHD, Oppositional Defiant Disorder, Conduct Disorder. No current
  medication. Low frustration tolerance; impulsive; can react with anger or crying.
- **Dislikes being corrected, ordered around, or having instructions repeated** —
  this triggers irritability. Design around it.
- Interests: soccer (plays defense), video games, swimming, drawing (pencil), music,
  reading including the Bible, loves cats.

## Both

- Siblings, very close bond. Spanish-speaking, seriously Catholic.
- Live in a majority-Latino neighborhood; their Cobb County (GA) schools are
  majority-Latino. American English, US school context.

## Differentiation rules (teacher-facing, must appear in every teacher-plan.md)

### Camilo

- **Minimize correction.** Use **recasts** (model the correct form back in
  conversation) instead of direct correction. Save any explicit correction for a
  brief, private post-task moment.
- Give instructions **once**, visually. Do not repeat instructions verbally.
- **Gamify** — points, soccer framing, short segments.
- **Prompt Camilo to answer first** in group questions so Luciana doesn't pre-empt
  him.

### Luciana

- Can handle **explicit grammar** and accuracy feedback.
- Give **visible goals** (today you will be able to…).

### Group dynamic

- **Do not let Luciana correct Camilo.** If she starts to, redirect gently — that's
  the teacher's job, not hers.
- Lower anxiety: personalize content to their interests; low-threat practice;
  frequent small wins; age-appropriate (they are 13 and 16 — not childish).
